I'll give you some general rules and some Ruby-specific suggestions. If you have any questions or want to chat about your job hunt, feel free to email me at jesse@codeunion.io! :)

Some General Rules for (Technical) Interviews


Be Humble
First, the mindset: humility. This is always true, but especially true when you're talking about your first job in a new field, entry-level or otherwise. You are not entitled to anything above and beyond common courtesy and basic respect for your time and person not an offer, not a nice interviewer, not fair questions, not anything.

You think coding on a whiteboard is stupid? So do I, but deal with it. You think questions about linked lists are stupid? You're only allowed to say that if you can articulate when they're the right data structure to use and when they're not. But even if you can, what's the upside in getting indignant?

Preempt Expectations
You're aspiring to be an entry-level Rails engineer. Maybe you went through a coding bootcamp. The cards are stacked against you, both because of your relative inexperience and because interviewers will already believe things about you, e.g., "They don't really know how to program, they only know how Rails works", "They might be able to cobble together a Rails application, but they don't really understand the fundamentals", "Anyone who went through a coding bootcamp is just looking for a shortcut to a well-paying job. They don't actually want to put in the effort."

These thoughts might strike you as unfair. They might even make you angry. Nevertheless, those thoughts are there and you have to filter everything you say and do through two lenses: (1) am I taking every opportunity to preempt these thoughts? and (2) am I unintentionally reinforcing these thoughts?

Maintain Momentum and Avoid "No"
Do everything you can to keep the process moving forward before, during, after the interview. This means doing things like...

  1. Arriving 30-ish minutes early to the location in case there are logistical "unknown unknowns," e.g., you can't find the front desk, your interviewer gave you bad directions and you find yourself walking in circles, etc.
  2. Bringing along multiple paper copies of your rsum (yes, paper). If someone asks, "Do you have a copy of your rsum?" you don't want to say, "No, sorry, I didn't print it out.", "No, sorry, I emailed it to so-and-so.", or "No, sorry, I only brought one copy and already gave it to someone."
  3. Pens. Pencils. Blank pieces of paper (lots). Markers. Does the interview room have a whiteboard but no whiteboard markers? Oops, it's the interviewer's fault, but you can still control the situation with a little preparation. This is better than wasting 10 minutes while the interviewer goes looking for some.
  4. Bring your laptop. It should be fully charged, but bring a power adapter regardless. Your laptop should set up to start coding immediately and you should verify that everything is working. Can you run code? Can you run Rails? Do it the night before.
  5. Whiteboards. There's a chance you'll be using them. Writing on a whiteboard isn't as easy as you might think, so do some practice runs. For example, you have to be sensitive to whether the way you're positioning your body is obscuring the interview's view of what you're writing. You also don't want to be a "whiteboard talker" and will have to learn how to write, talk, and look at your interviewer at the same time. These are Presentation Skills 101, honestly, but they still requires practice.
  6. Talk. Speak. Ramble. Use whatever words come to mind, even if you're afraid they'll make you sound stupid. If you say 20 things in the span of 10 minutes and 5 are smart, a good interviewer will hone in on those. If you say 2 things in the span of 10 minutes and only one is smart, not only will the interviewer be frustrated with the rhythm of the interview but they'll have very little to hone in on. "I can't tell." and "This person is dangerously incompetent." have the same outcome for you: no offer.


Memorize and Practice Essential Patterns
Every (modern) language shares three core flow control constructs: iteration, branching, and subroutines (i.e., functions & methods). Know the patterns for these things inside and out. You should also know your language's core data types (booleans, integers, floats, strings, arrays, hashes, etc.) and, if your language supports them, objects and classes.

No, you don't need to memorize every last syntactical detail of Ruby or JavaScript or whatever else, but if you can't remember how to write a for loop in JavaScript and have to look it up, it will slow down the interview and send a signal that you don't know what you're doing, don't write code enough to be comfortable with these patterns, or didn't know enough to prepare.

Ruby-Specific Questions

This will be more laundry list style. Everything above applies here, obviously, so I'm not doing to say things like "You should be able to define and call a methods." See Memorize and Practice Essential Patterns above.

Here's an example question, in three parts.

  1. # 1. Implement your own Enumerable#map 
  2. def map(array, &block) 
  3. end 
  4.  
  5. # 2. Implement your own Enumerable#inject 
  6. def inject(initial, array, &block) 
  7. end 
  8.  
  9. # 3. Now, re-implement your map method 
  10. # as a single call to (your) inject 

  1. Have a consistent, idiomatic style. I like GitHub's Ruby Style Guide. Some things are non-negotiable and if you don't do them it will send a funny signal (e.g., Ruby code is always always always indented with two spaces per indentation level not tabs, not 4 spaces, but 2 spaces). Other things are more negotiable, but it's nice to see candidates who care that their code is easy for other people to read.
  2. Know how to read and navigation the documentation at http://ruby-doc.org/.
  3. Ruby has many ways to iterate. Know them. while ... do ... end , enum.each { |...| ... }, Integer#upto, Integer#downto, and Integer#times are often the most direct way of expressing the kind of loop you want to do. Obviously each is the most important.
  4. Blocks. Know how to identify them using both the { |a,b,c| ...} syntax and the do |a,b,c| ... end syntax. Know how to write methods that accept blocks as arguments using both the yield[/style] syntax and [code]&block / block.call syntax.
  5. Be comfortable with core Enumerable types, how to iterate over them, and when it is appropriate to use each one. That means being comfortable with Array, Hash, and Range at a minimum. Bonus points for using Set where appropriate.
  6. Enumerable methods. At a bare minimum you should know how to use, implement, and explain the workings of Enumerable#each_with_index, Enumerable#inject, Enumerable#map, and Enumerable#select. You should be able to spot code that can be refactored using these methods. If you want to go deeper, understand how to implement map and select using inject.
  7. Classes and objects. Be able to define them. Understand how instance variables work. Understand why class variables in Ruby, i.e., @@class_variable, are almost never what you want. Understand what attr_read, attr_writer, and attr_accessor do. Better yet: be able to implement your own versions of each. Understand SOLID (object-oriented design) and how it applies to Ruby code. The Law Of Demeter is your friend.


General Web and Rails-Specific Questions


Mostly you have to understand what's "really happening." I'm a big fan of giving candidates a pre-built Rails application with bugs and other issues and asking them to fix the problems.

  1. Understand the HTTP request/response cycle. Do you know what an HTTP request looks like? It's all human-readable and in plain text, so the answer to this is very concrete. Have you even seen a raw HTTP request before?
  2. Understand the difference between "the internet" and "the web"
  3. Be able to answer the ol' clich: "Tell me what happens when you type http://codeunion.io into your browser's URL bar and press enter."
  4. ActiveRecord: know how it relates to SQL and how to use it idiomatically. See jfarmer/theory_of_learning.md for an overview of ActiveRecord. Don't be sloppy with validations and associations, either.
  5. Schema design: know how to design a properly normalized database schema and how to identify whether a database schema is well-designed or not.
  6. Form helpers and nested forms. They kind of suck, but they're everywhere in Rails-land.
  7. Know common bugs like "What is the n+1 selects issue?" Avoid them and know how to identify and verify them when they're present.
  8. Know where all the parts of a Rails application live. What goes where and why?
  9. Know what aspects of Rails are there for conventional or historical reasons, especially if they're part of the "Rails magic," e.g., auto-pluralizing model names to derive the underlying database table name.


As a potential entry-level engineer, you are ignorant, whether you know it or not. This means anyone hiring you knows they're taking on the responsibility of teaching you a bunch of stuff.

Between someone who knows Rails inside and out but doesn't know what's "really happening" and someone who is shaky with the details of Rails but understands what's happening under the hood, an employer will prefer the latter. Why? The assumption will be that it's easier to teach the latter person Rails than it is to teach the former person the fundamentals.

Hope that helps!

What skills are required by a junior ruby on rails developer in order to get a job?

You are expected to be familiar with the following topics

  1. Object Oriented Programming in Ruby
  2. Active Record CRUD Operations, Validations, Associations & Call backs
  3. Routes, Restful Architecture, Controllers
  4. Views, Forms, Links, tables
  5. Gems like Devise (authentication), Pundit (Authorization), Chartkick (Charts), RABL (Web Service), Sidekiq (background jobs), Paperclip (file upload)
  6. Web Services like JSON and working with APIs
  7. Deployment
  8. Version Control using Git
  9. UI Framework - Bootstrap
  10. Web Development good practices
  11. Being familiar of jQuery & Ajax is also beneficial
